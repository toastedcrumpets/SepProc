Distillation: Part 02

  • In the previous lecture we covered flash drums.
  • The design process for these distillation units is very similar to a single stage absorber.
  • But this time, we continued to use the concept of an operating line, and to use this along with the VLE data to solve the design graphically.
  • A numerical solution was also possible using the relative volatility.
  • We will consider a difficult system to solve graphically, known as differential distillation or stills or batch distillation.
  • Batch distillation is used whenever batch processes are viable.
  • Usually these are for small-scale, high-value, and/or highly controlled (i.e., medical or edible) or “traditional” products.
  • An example product which meets all of the above conditions as a small-scale high-value traditional native scottish medicine is in the distillation of whiskey in pot stills (see right).
  • In this process, a volume of slightly alcoholic “ mash ” is boiled and the ethanol-rich vapour separated, without any fresh feed being added to the still.
  • Only a limited amount of vapour is collected, leaving the majority of the undesired products in the liquid phase.
  • The first vapour produced from the still (the “head cut”) is discarded as this contains lighter alcohols (including small amounts of methanol which may make you blind). These are more volatile than ethanol and so the mixture boils at a low temperature while they are present (<78 ${}^\circ$C)
  • Once the lighter alcohols have been evaporated and the vapour temperature reaches around 78 ${}^\circ$C, the so-called “Heart of the run” vapour is collected.
  • This vapour may get up to 70% in ethanol and is either passed to another stage of batch distillation (typical) or is collected for maturation in oak casks.
  • Once a significant amount of the ethanol in the original liquid (and flavours) has been collected, the temperature begins to rise as heavier alcohols begin to boil.
  • These alcohols are the so-called fusel alcohols, which is German for bad-liquor alcohols (English equivalents are rotgut or hooch).
  • The idea that temperature is related to concentration/composition is essential in distillation (think about your upcoming lab!).
  • The distillation is stopped at this point and the remaining “tail cut” liquid may be combined with the “head cut” and recycled to the next distillation (as there is still ethanol in there) or discarded. If it is recycled, there must be at least some discard (see your reactors course!).
  • As process engineers, this is an interesting example of a batch multicomponent distillation, where vapour is only collected at the certain temperature range where a desirable component boils.
  • As process engineers, we are also interested in how we might model this process.
  • The main complication is the dynamic nature of the process (i.e., how it changes in time).
  • Initially, $L_{initial}$ moles of feed liquid are charged to the still.
  • This is then boiled until a desired number of moles, $V$, has vapourised, leaving $L_{final}$ moles of liquid remaining in the still.
  • Just as for the flash drum, we can create two balance equations, one in the total mass and one in the mass of the most volatile component. \begin{align*} L_{initial}&=L_{final}+V\\ x_{initial} L_{initial} &= x_{final} L_{final}+y_{avg} V \end{align*}
  • But it is key to note that the average vapour concentration $y_{avg}$ is not in equilibrium with the remaining liquid!
  • We cannot use this equation as an operating line as $y_{avg}$ bears no direct relation to $y$.
  • At first, the liquid has a high concentration of volatile component $A$, and so it produces a high concentration vapour.
  • This high concentration vapour is removed and collected using a condenser so that more liquid is allowed to boil.
  • By the end of the distillation, the concentration of the volatile component in the liquid has dropped.
  • This is because the volatile component has boiled away faster than the less volatile components.
  • Thus, the average vapour concentration, $y_{avg}$ is higher than the equilibrium vapour concentration $y$ at the end of the distillation.
  • If we want to determine actual concentration at any point in time, we will need to look at a differential balance (See EX3030).
  • Assume the still is in operation. We have $L$ moles of liquid in the still with a concentration of $x$.
  • In a small instant of time, ${\rm d}L$ moles of liquid boil to vapour of concentration $y$.
  • This reduces the amount of liquid by ${\rm d}L$, and the concentration of the liquid by ${\rm d}x$
  • Performing a mass balance between these two instants of time, we have \begin{align*} x L &= \left(x-{\rm d}x\right)\left(L-{\rm d}L\right) + y {\rm d}L \end{align*}
  • We can cancel the ${\rm d}x {\rm d}L$ term as it is small, and we have \begin{align*} x L &= x L-L {\rm d}x - x {\rm d}L + y {\rm d}L \end{align*}
  • With some rearrangement, we have \begin{align*} \frac{{\rm d}L}{L}&= \frac{{\rm d}x}{y-x} \end{align*}
  • Integrating both sides, we have \begin{align*} \int_{L_{initial}}^{L_{final}}\frac{{\rm d} L}{L}&= \int_{x_{initial}}^{x_{final}}\frac{{\rm d}x}{y-x}\\ \ln\left(\frac{L_{final}}{L_{initial}}\right)&= \int_{x_{initial}}^{x_{final}}\frac{{\rm d}x}{y-x} \end{align*}
  • This final equation is known as Rayleigh's equation for batch or differential distillation, and is our operating balance equation.
  • It should be no surprise that differential distillation requires differential equations to solve it!
  • But the right hand side is not in a usable form… yet.
\begin{align*} \ln\left(\frac{L_{final}}{L_{initial}}\right)&= \int_{x_{initial}}^{x_{final}}\frac{{\rm d}x}{y-x} \end{align*}
  • To solve this equation, we need to perform the integration on the right hand side.
  • To complete this term, we need a relation between $y$ and $x$, which are in equilibrium
    $\to$ VLE data!
  • We can actually do this if we assume an approximately constant relative volatility and use the following equation. \begin{align*} y &= \frac{\alpha x}{1 + x (\alpha - 1)} \end{align*}
  • The solution is \begin{align*} \ln\left(\frac{L_{final}}{L_{initial}}\right) = \left(\alpha-1\right)^{-1}\ln\left(\frac{x_{final}(1-x_{initial})}{x_{initial}(1-x_{final})}\right) + \ln\left(\frac{1-x_{initial}}{1-x_{final}}\right) \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \ln\left(\frac{L_{final}}{L_{initial}}\right)&= \int_{x_{initial}}^{x_{final}}\frac{{\rm d}x}{y-x} \end{align*}
  • But graphical methods are all the rage! Can we solve this graphically for any $x$-$y$ data?
  • Let's try and batch distill a 100 kmol Benzene-Tolulene mixture of $x_{initial}=0.6$.
  • We will boil the liquid until it reaches a concentration of $x_{final}=0.1$.
  • How much liquid is remaining in the still, and what is the average concentration of the collected vapour?
    \begin{align*} L_{initial} &=100 \text{kmol} & L_{final} &=? & V&=?\\ x_{initial}&=0.6 & x_{final}&=0.1 & y_{avg}&=? \end{align*}
\begin{align*} L_{initial} &=100 \text{kmol} & L_{final} &=? & V&=?\\ x_{initial}&=0.6 & x_{final}&=0.1 & y_{avg}&=? \end{align*}
  • First, we will try to determine $L_{final}$ using the Rayleigh equation. \begin{align*} \ln\left(\frac{L_{final}}{L_{initial}}\right)&= \int_{x_{initial}}^{x_{final}}\frac{{\rm d}x}{y-x} \end{align*}
  • We want to determine the right hand side of the equation so that we can calculate $L_{final}$.
  • We will look at two graphical methods of doing this.
\begin{align*} L_{initial} &=100 \text{kmol} & L_{final} &=? & V&=?\\ x_{initial}&=0.6 & x_{final}&=0.1 & y_{avg}&=? \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \ln\left(\frac{L_{final}}{L_{initial}}\right)&= \int_{x_{initial}}^{x_{final}}\frac{{\rm d}x}{y-x} \end{align*}
  • Introducing, The Brute Force Method:
    • Use the $x$ - $y$ VLE data to plot another graph on graph paper, but plot $(y-x)^{-1}$ versus $x$.
    • The right hand side of the above equation is then equal to the area under the curve between $x=x_{initial}$ and $x=x_{final}$.
  • Let's see that in action…
\begin{align*} L_{initial} &=100 \text{kmol} & L_{final} &=? & V&=?\\ x_{initial}&=0.6 & x_{final}&=0.1 & y_{avg}&=? \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \ln\left(\frac{L_{final}}{L_{initial}}\right)&= \int_{x_{initial}}^{x_{final}}\frac{{\rm d}x}{y-x} \end{align*}
  • 659 red squares, each with an area of $0.02\times0.2$, giving a total area of \begin{align*} \int_{x_{initial}}^{x_{final}}\frac{{\rm d}x}{y-x} \approx -0.02\times0.2\times659 \approx -2.636 \end{align*}
  • The area is negative as $x_{final}<x_{initial}$ (we're integrating backwards).
  • This was very tedious to count, and is a very ancient, noble, and tedious way of graphically calculating the integral of an unknown function.
  • We now use numerical techniques such as Trapezoidal/Simpson's rule (see your reactors course) to calculate integrals which we cannot solve analytically.
  • Once we've done this, can we plot something easier to use instead?
\begin{align*} L_{initial} &=100 \text{kmol} & L_{final} &=? & V&=?\\ x_{initial}&=0.6 & x_{final}&=0.1 & y_{avg}&=? \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \ln\left(\frac{L_{final}}{L_{initial}}\right)&= \int_{x_{initial}}^{x_{final}}\frac{{\rm d}x}{y-x} \end{align*}
  • We note that \begin{align*} \int_{x_1}^{x_2} f(x){\rm d}x = \int_{0}^{x_2} f(x){\rm d}x -\int_{0}^{x_1} f(x){\rm d}x \end{align*}
  • So we could just plot $\int_{0}^{x}\left(y-x\right)^{-1}{\rm d}x$ versus $x$, once, for a set of VLE data and use the above identity to design any differential distillation of that mixture.
\begin{align*} L_{initial} &=100 \text{kmol} & L_{final} &=? & V&=?\\ x_{initial}&=0.6 & x_{final}&=0.1 & y_{avg}&=? \end{align*}
  • From counting the squares underneath the graph, we obtained \begin{align*} \int_{x_{initial}}^{x_{final}}\frac{{\rm d}x}{y-x}\approx -2.636 \end{align*}
  • Using the pre-integrated equation plot, we obtained \begin{align*} \int_{x_{initial}}^{x_{final}}\frac{{\rm d}x}{y-x}\approx -2.62 \end{align*}
  • Both methods give nearly the same answer, but the second method is far easier (if the graph is available), and potentially more accurate.
\begin{align*} L_{initial} &=100 \text{kmol} & L_{final} &=? & V&=?\\ x_{initial}&=0.6 & x_{final}&=0.1 & y_{avg}&=? \end{align*}
  • We can now solve this equation for $L_{final}$ \begin{align*} \ln\left(\frac{L_{final}}{L_{initial}}\right)&= \int_{x_{initial}}^{x_{final}}\frac{{\rm d}x}{y-x}\\ \ln\left(\frac{L_{final}}{100}\right)&\approx -2.62\\ L_{final} &\approx 100 e^{-2.62} \approx 7.28 \end{align*}
  • This means the amount of vapour produced is \begin{align*} V = L_{initial}-L_{final} = 100 - 7.28 = 92.72 \end{align*}
\begin{align*} L_{initial} &=100 \text{kmol} & L_{final} &= 7.28 & V&=92.72\\ x_{initial}&=0.6 & x_{final}&=0.1 & y_{avg}&=? \end{align*}
  • We can calculate $y_{avg}$ from the benzene mass balance \begin{align*} x_{initial} L_{initial} &= x_{final} L_{final}+y_{avg} V\\ 0.6\times100 &= 0.1 \times 7.28 + 92.72 y_{avg}\\ y_{avg} \approx 0.64 \end{align*}
  • This highlights that when using batch distillation to achieve a high separation, only a small volume of liquid should be boiled, otherwise we don't achieve a significant separation.
  • In whisky distillation, only 10-15% of the wash is actually vapourised in the still for each batch.
  • Here, we boiled 92.72% of the initial charge of the still.
  • Perhaps multi-stage distillation will be more efficient at achieving high purities AND high recovery?
