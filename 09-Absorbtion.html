Absorbtion

  • So far, we have only considered systems where there is a perfect separation.
  • This has let us revise mass/energy balances, enthalpy calculations and boiling point rises.
  • But we are limited to extremes of volatility and solubility. The sugar/salt/NaOH is not present in the vapour phase and always dissolves in the liquid phase.
  • We will need to consider components that are soluble in both phases of the process streams.
  • Consider the simple binary and more complex multi-component distillation above.
  • We will need data or expressions that describes the relative distribution of the components of the system between the different phases.
  • The first relation we will discuss is Gibb's phase rule.
  • Gibb's phase rule is a function which states the number of independent intensive properties (e.g., Temperature, Pressure), also called degrees of freedom, which are needed to completely specify an equilibrium mixture of fluids.
  • It is expressed as follows: \begin{align*} F = C-P+2 \end{align*} where $F$ is the degrees of freedom, $C$ is the number of components in the system, and $P$ is the number of phases in the system.

Single Component Systems

\begin{align*} F = C-P+2 \end{align*}
  • For example, in a single phase ($P=1$), single component ( $C=1$) system like water, we need $F=2$ variables to completely specify its state (e.g. pressure and temperature).
  • If we then start to boil water, the number of phases increases to two ($P=2$), reducing the degree's of freedom to $F=1$ !
  • This is because you can either specify the temperature or pressure of a boiling fluid (e.g., steam), but you cannot control each independently.
  • This explains why Dühring charts can have water's boiling temperature as an “effective” pressure axis.

Multi-Component Systems

\begin{align*} F = C-P+2 \end{align*}
  • Let's take a fluid with an extra component like our sugar or NaOH solutions ($C=2$).
  • If we are boiling this solution we will have two phases ($P=2$), and the number of degrees of freedom is $F=2$.
  • We can now (depending on the BPR) specify the temperature and pressure of the system, even though it is boiling, as this fixes the concentration of the fluid.
  • We may set the temperature and pressure, or the pressure and concentration, or the concentration and temperature, but not all three.

Multi-Component Systems

\begin{align*} F = C-P+2 \end{align*}
  • Your experience of designing a multi-stage evaporator should give you a feel for this. You specify the temperature and concentration and calculate the pressure.
  • Gibb's phase rule is interesting to understand how many variables are required to be solved in a system (similar to a degrees of freedom analysis), but it does not directly help you to solve the separation problem.
  • We need more equations (or laws) which describe the distribution of components between the phases.
  • Much of our designs will consider experimental data in the form of Vapour-Liquid Equilibrium ( VLE) Data.
  • On the right we have a temperature-concentration diagram which can be used to calculate the boiling point and concentrations of Benzene-Tolulene mixtures.
  • Benzene is more volatile than Tolulene, which we can exploit in a separation process.
  • When solving the mass balances of the system, we often discard the temperature data and generate $x$ - $y$ VLE diagrams as above.
  • These are more convenient when using graphical methods to solve the mass balance of a separation process.
  • In the limit of an ideal mixture or high/low concentrations there are useful law's which you may use if appropriate for your system.
  • For ideal mixtures (AKA mixtures of homologous compounds), or when you are looking at the vapour pressure of a component at large concentrations, Raoult's law holds…
  • c Raoult's law was discovered in 1882 by Fran ois-Marie Raoult, and is given by \begin{align*} p_A = P_A x_A \end{align*} where $p_A$ is the partial pressure, $P_A$ is the vapour pressure, and $x_A$ is the mole fraction of component $A$.
  • This allows you to estimate the molar concentrations of components in the gas phase if you know the total pressure using Dalton's law: \begin{align*} y_A = \frac{p_A}{P} = \frac{P_A x_A}{P} \end{align*}
  • Unmodified Raoult's law is of limited value as it does not have a wide range of application (see right) except for ideal mixtures, where similar compounds are mixed (e.g., hexane-heptane, benzene-toluene) or large concentrations of $A$ ( $x_A\to1$, region $C\to D$).
  • We can modify Raoult's law to extend it to non-ideal solutions \begin{align*} p_A \phi_A = P_A \gamma_A x_A \end{align*} where $\phi_A$ is the fugacity coefficient and $\gamma_A$ is the activity coefficient.
  • The fugacity coefficient corrects for non-ideal gas behaviour, but many gases are effectively ideal for the industrially relevant states $\phi_A=1$.
  • The activity coefficient corrects for non-ideal liquid interactions, and will need to be obtained from experimental values or empirical fits.
  • Its values have meaning at the molecular scale:
    • $\gamma < 1$ : The two molecule types strongly attract each other compared to their own type, decreasing their volatility.
    • $\gamma > 1$ : Molecules strongly repel each other compared to their own type, increasing their volatility.
  • I expect you to be familiar with calculation of VLE data from activity coefficients (e.g., the van Laar model): \begin{align} \ln \gamma_A = A_{AB} \left(\frac{A_{BA} x_B}{A_{AB} x_A+A_{BA} x_B}\right)^2 \end{align}
  • And Antoine data: \begin{align} \log_{10} p_A &= A_A - \frac{B_A}{C_A+T}+… \end{align}
  • Where $A_A,B_A,C_A$ are empirically determined coefficients for a given component $A$, and $A_{AB},A_{BA}$ are empirically determined interaction coefficients between component $A$ and $B$.
  • If this is unfamiliar, please re-read your thermodynamics notes or see “Introduction to Chemical Engineering Thermodynamics” by Smith, van Ness, and Abbott.
  • At the limit of low concentration ($A\to B$), we can use Henry's law.
  • Henry's law was discovered by William Henry in 1803, and is written as \begin{align*} p_A = \mathcal{H}_A x_a \end{align*}
  • where $\mathcal{H}_A$ is the Henry's law constant and is the analog of the vapour pressure in Raoult's law.
  • In fact, for ideal mixtures we have: \begin{align*} \mathcal{H}_A & = \mathcal{P}_A & \text{(Ideal mixtures)} \end{align*}
  • This is not often true, for example, although gases such as N${}_2$ /O ${}_2$ /CO ${}_2$ have enormous vapour pressures at $25{}^\circ$ C, they disolve to a measurable amount in water due to strong interactions ( $\mathcal{H}_A \ll P_A$) and Henry's law describes this.
  • Henry's law is very useful in systems such as gas absorbers where only a low concentration of the gas is dissolved in the absorbing liquid.
  • One example of a gas absorber is the aerator in an aquarium, responsible for absorbing $O_2$ and desorbing $CO_2$.
  • Another example are fizzy-drink carbonators (sodastream for lab scale, coca-cola for industrial scale) where CO${}_2$ is absorbed into water.
  • But Henry's law cannot be used out of its limiting concentration, it is restricted to approximating the interaction of almost isolated molecules of $A$ in a fluid of $B$.
  • What will be the concentration of oxygen dissolved in water at 298 K when the solution is in equilibrium with air at 1 atm total pressure?
  • The partial pressure of oxygen in air is given by Dalton's law: \begin{align*} p_{O_2} = P y_{O_2} = 1\times0.21 = 0.21 \textrm{atm} \end{align*}
  • Using this in Henry's law with the coefficient of $\mathcal{H}_A=4.259\times10^4$ atm at $25{}^\circ$ C (note there can be many different units!), we have \begin{align*} p_{O_2} &= \mathcal{H}_A  x_{O_2}\\ x_{O_2} &= p_{O_2}/\mathcal{H}_A = 0.21 / 4.259\times10^4 = 4.80\times10^{-6} \text{mol %} \end{align*}
  • This is a remarkably low concentration of oxygen considering the scale of aerobic life within the ocean.
  • Sharks extract up to 80% of the oxygen in the water that passes over their gils, whereas we only extract 25% of the oxygen in the air which passes through our lungs (what about air breathing fish?).
  • Some shark species (great white shark, whale shark) must keep swimming to ensure they process sufficient volumes of water, otherwise they can “drown.”
  • Solubility decreases significantly with temperature, e.g. (14.6 mg/L at 0${}^\circ$ C versus 7.6 mg/L at 20 ${}^\circ$ C), hence the environmental restrictions on the temperature of cooling water dumped into local waterways.
  • Let's consider a single-stage gas absorber.
  • In such a device, two immiscible fluids (a gas and a liquid) are contacted and mixed thoroughly.
  • We will consider the absorption of $CO_2$ from a $CO_2$ -air mixture using pure water as an example.
  • Within the phases at inlet, the CO${}_2$ component of the gas phase is out of equilibrium with the liquid phase, but it is brought into equilibrium within the absorber.
  • An idealised diagram of a single-stage absorber is presented on the right.
  • We assume the two exit streams are in equilibrium, as the absorber is well-mixed.
  • Although the numbering scheme for the streams is a little odd, it will make sense when we look at multi-stage absorbtion.
  • Each stream is numbered according to the stage it was produced from, so we are looking at the first stage of a multi-stage absorber.
  • Performing a total mass balance over the absorber, we have \begin{align*} L_0+V_2 = L_1+V_1=M \end{align*} where $M$ is the total mass flow-rate.
  • Labelling our components A=$CO_2$, B=air and C=water for generality, we can perform mass balances in each component \begin{align*} L_0 x_{A,0}+V_2 y_{A,2}&=L_1 x_{A,1} + V_1 y_{A,1} = M x_{AM}\\ L_0 x_{B,0}+V_2 y_{B,2}&=L_1 x_{B,1} + V_1 y_{B,1} = M x_{BM} \end{align*} where $x_{AM}$ and $x_{BM}$ are the average fractions of air and $CO_2$ respectively.
  • There is no need to perform a balance in $x_{C}$, as we have the relationships \begin{align*} x_{A,\alpha}+x_{B,\alpha}+x_{C,\alpha} &= 1 & \text{for $\alpha\in0,1 $}\\ y_{A,\beta}+y_{B,\beta}+y_{C,\beta} &= 1 & \text{for $\beta\in1,2 $}\\ \end{align*}
  • We can make some simplifying assumptions in our system.
  • We assume that air is essentially insoluble in the water phase and that water does not vaporise to the gas phase.
  • This is a massive simplification, but is justified if there is significant pressures of CO${}_2$, and the water was in equilibirum with 100% humidity air before coming into contact.
  • This implies that the vapour stream is a binary A-B ($CO_2$ -air) stream, and the liquid is a binary A-C ( $CO_2$ -water) stream.
  • We can then rewrite our balance for A=$CO_2$, in terms of the inert stream flow-rates, water ( $L'$) and air ( $V'$). \begin{multline*} L'\frac{x_{A,0}}{1-x_{A,0}}+V'\frac{y_{A,2}}{1-y_{A,2}}\\ = L'\frac{x_{A,1}}{1-x_{A,1}}+V'\frac{y_{A,1}}{1-y_{A,1}} \\ = M x_{AM} \end{multline*}
\begin{align*} L'\frac{x_{A,0}}{1-x_{A,0}}+V'\frac{y_{A,2}}{1-y_{A,2}} = L'\frac{x_{A,1}}{1-x_{A,1}}+V'\frac{y_{A,1}}{1-y_{A,1}} = M x_{AM} \end{align*}
  • The above equation is our balance equation for a single absorber stage with the assumption that there is only one absorbing/desorping component.
  • From the specification of the inlet stream compositions and flow rates, we can calculate the flow-rates of water and air using \begin{align*} L'&=L_0\left(1-x_{A,0}\right) & V'&=V_2\left(1-y_{A,2}\right) \end{align*}
