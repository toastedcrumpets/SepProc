Distillation: Part 1

  • So far, we've relied on Henry's law, or experimental solubility charts to calculate the distribution of material between two phases.
  • This data is limited to low liquid concentrations of the solute (see right).
  • This low concentration limitation is not a problem when considering absorption
  • But at higher liquid concentrations, the dissolved component's vapour pressure changes, influencing the overall pressure (or temperature).
  • In distillation processes, the separated compounds are both volatile and separations are usually carried out over the whole range of liquid concentrations ($A\to D$).
  • We will need to obtain full Vapour-Liquid Equilibrium (VLE) data from $x=0$ to $x=1$ for distillation design.
  • But the amount of experimental data available on mixtures is limited (we cannot look up curves for all mixtures, pressures, and temperatures in a book). What experimental data we have also needs interpolation.
  • We will need to look at methods of generating $x$-$y$ charts from theoretical relationships based on the properties of pure liquids, or using advanced equations of state before we can do distillation design.
  • We need ($T$-)$x$-$y$ diagrams at a fixed pressure as we usually assume an isobaric process for the stage calculations.
  • When we begin to design a separation column, we usually assume a fixed operating pressure as the system is relatively well-connected.
  • These columns are essentially pipes with a counter-current multiphase flow, so there will be a pressure drop causing a variation in the overall pressure. We must neglect this at first to perform a rough sizing.
  • Given a pressure and a range of liquid concentrations, we need to work out the vapour concentrations AND boiling point.
  • Can we create $T$ - $x$ - $y$ diagrams from Raoult's law? \begin{align*} p_A(T, x_A) = P_A(T) x_A \end{align*} where $p_A$ is the partial pressure, $P_A$ is the vapour pressure, and $x_A$ is the mole fraction of component $A$.
  • The pressure of a boiling ideal mixture is given by a combination of Dalton's law and Raoult's law: \begin{align} \label{eq:pressure} P(T, x_A, x_B,…) = \sum_i p_i(T, x_i)=\sum_i P_i(T) x_i \end{align}
  • Literature data for vapour pressures are often correlated using the Antoine equation \begin{align} \label{eq:antione} \log_{10} P_A(T) = k_1 - \frac{k_2}{k_3+T}+… \end{align}
  • So, if we specify mixture concentrations at a fixed pressure, we must solve the above equations for the boiling temperature before we can find the partial pressures!
  • If we had an isothermal process, it would be a lot easier to create P-x-y diagrams, but unfortunately this is a rare case.
  • Once the boiling pressure is known, we can write: \begin{align*} y_A(T,x_A,x_B,…) = \frac{p_A(T,x_A)}{P(T,x_A,x_B,…)} = \frac{P_A(T) x_A}{\sum_i P_i(T) x_i} \end{align*}
  • This is a useful expression, as the vapour pressures $P_A$ of pure components are widely available in literature and straightforward to measure in experiment.
  • We can generate $T$-$x$-$y$ graphs, and we even have an expression which is valid for multicomponent ideal mixtures!
  • There are many cases where we have close-to- ideal mixtures, such as benzene-tolulene, or hexane-heptane, and we can use the above equation.
  • But this equation requires solving for the pressure, which is not trivial without the use of a computer. Are there any simpler approaches available?
\begin{align*} y_A = \frac{P_A x_A}{\sum_i P_i x_i} \end{align*}
  • Is there a way to simplify the above equation so that we don't have to solve for the temperature to generate the $x$ - $y$ VLE data?
  • In a binary system, we can expand the sum in the equation above, replace $x_B=1-x_A$, and divide the top and bottom by $P_B$ to give \begin{align*} y_A &= \frac{P_A x_A}{P_A x_A + P_B x_B}\\ &= \frac{\frac{P_A}{P_B} x_A}{\frac{P_A}{P_B} x_A +1 - x_A}\\ &= \frac{\alpha x_A}{1 + x_A (\alpha - 1)} \end{align*} where $\alpha=P_A/P_B$ is the relative volatility !
\begin{align*} y_A &= \frac{\alpha x_A}{1 + x_A (\alpha - 1)} \end{align*}
  • The advantage of using the relative volatility, $\alpha$, over the partial pressures $P_A/P_B$ is that it remains remarkably steady for many systems.
  • For example, values are given below for benzene-tolulene mixtures
  • Although the relative volatility varies over the range, provided the values at the top and bottom of the column differ less than 15%, we can use this for our rough initial design.
  • When drawing the equilibrium line, we would use the average relative volatility over the whole column.
  • A high ($\alpha\gg 1$) or low ( $\alpha\to0$) value of the relative volatility is desirable, as this makes separation easier (higher concentrations of the volatile component in the vapour phase).
  • If $\alpha\approx1$, we cannot use distillation to separate these components, as the vapour and liquid phases would have equal concentrations.
  • For Benzene-Tolulene, $\alpha$ increases as the boiling temperature decreases.
  • Lower boiling temperatures usually implies lower pressures. This shows that it can be more efficient to distill Benzene-Tolulene at reduced pressures (also known as vacuum distillation).
  • In summary, we can use Raoult's law for ideal mixtures to generate $x$ - $y$ diagrams. Some ideal mixtures have roughly constant relative volatility, making it even simpler to solve. Mixtures with a relative volatility approaching one are difficult to separate using distillation.
  • But what about non-ideal systems?
  • In this case we need to use something like modified Raoult's law: \begin{align} p_A(T, x_A)=\gamma_A(x_A, x_B) P_A(T) x_A \end{align} (I've assumed the fugacity coefficient is one)
  • To calculate the activity coefficient we need a semi-empirical relationship, such as the van-Laar or Margules expressions: \begin{align*} \gamma_A &= \exp\left[x^2_B\left(\mathcal{A}_{AB} + 2 x_A\left[\mathcal{A}_{BA} -\mathcal{A}_{AB}\right]\right)\right] & \text{Margules-3} \\ \gamma_A &=\exp\left[\frac{\mathcal{A}_{AB}}{1 + \left(\mathcal{A}_{AB} x_A / \mathcal{A}_{BA} x_B\right)}\right] & \text{van-Laar-2} \end{align*}
  • Although these expressions look complicated, they are just functions of the mixture concentrations $x_A$ and a set of interaction constants ( $\mathcal{A}_{AB}$) which you look up for each fluid pairing.
  • Now that we can generate VLE ($T$-)$x$-$y$ diagrams and data, we can look at designing various distillation processes.
  • The simplest piece of distillation equipment to design (if we ignore the heat balance) is the flash drum (also called a knockout drum).
  • A feed liquid is typically boiled by a sudden pressure drop through a pressure valve or through a nozzle.
  • This may be preceded by a preheater to increase the amount of vapour produced.
  • A liquid stream may “flash” whenever a pressure drop occurs (See BLEVE-vid1, BLEVE-vid2, BLEVE-vid3, Los Alfaques disaster), but a flash drum is a specific piece of equipment in which the flash boiling is designed to occur, and the liquid is allowed to settle out of the vapour stream.
  • Please check out the Chemical Safety Board's YouTube channel.
  • We need an operating line equation for this system.
  • We can perform a total mass balance, but this equation doesn't tell us anything about the concentrations in the system. \begin{align*} F=L+V \end{align*}
  • A mass balance in a single component is much more useful, and when taken over the volatile component $A$, it forms the operating line equation. \begin{align*} x_{A,F} F=x_A L+y_A V \end{align*}
  • As in absorbers, we assume that the outlet vapour and liquid streams are in equilibrium. \begin{align*} y_A = f^{(VLE)}(x_A) \end{align*}
\begin{align*} F&=L+V\\ x_{A,F} F&=x_A L+y_A V\\ y_A &= f^{(VLE)}(x_A) \end{align*}
  • Given the design of the flash drum, the pressure and inlet fluid temperature (pre-pressure drop) are the controlled/input variables of the design.
  • We will need an enthalpy balance to actually close this equation (to calculate how much vapour is produced).
  • However, to simplify this system at first we will assume we know the fraction of vapour produced.
  • A high-pressure liquid of 73 kmol/hr Benzene ($A$) and 137 kmol/hr Toluene ( $B$) is separated in a flash drum. The operating pressure is controlled so that the outlet vapour flow-rate is 100 kmol/hr. Calculate the outlet concentrations and flow-rates.
  • The total flow rate is $F=73+137=210$ kmol/hr. The liquid flow rate is then given by $L=F-V=210-100=110$ kmol/hr.
  • The volatile component is Benzene. The operating line equation is given by the Benzene mass balance. \begin{align*} x_{A,F} F&=x_A L+y_A V\\ 73 &= 110 x_A+100 y_A\\ y_A &= 0.73 - 1.1 x_A \end{align*}
  • Plotting the operating line, we find the solution for the outlet concentrations: $x_A=0.26$, $y_A=0.45$.
  • We can also solve this problem using our new relative volatility VLE equation…
  • We don't know the operating temperature of the flash drum as we've ignored the enthalpy balance for now, so we will just guess at a relative volatility of $\alpha=2.4$.
  • We then solve two equations, the operating line equation, and the constant relative volatility VLE data equation: \begin{align*} y_A &= \frac{\alpha x_A}{1 + x_A (\alpha - 1)} & y_A &= 0.73 - 1.1 x_A \end{align*}
  • We obtain a quadratic, where one root is ($x_A=-1.86$, $y_A=2.78$) and the other root is ( $x_A=0.25$, $y_A=0.45$)
  • The first root is obviously unphysical, but the second root is very close to the graphical solution ($x_A=0.26$, $y_A=0.45$).
  • Given the graphical method's answer ($y_A=0.45$, $x_A=0.26$) What is the magnitude of the error of assuming a constant value of $\alpha=2.4$ for the relative volatility in this process?
  • We can do a sensitivity analysis and test how $x_A$ and $y_A$ depend on the value of $\alpha$.
  • For $\alpha=2.6$, solving the previous two equations yields ( $y_A=0.46$, $x_A=0.25$).
  • For $\alpha=2.4$, solving the previous two equations yields ($y_A=0.45$, $x_A=0.26$,).
  • For $\alpha=2.2$, solving the previous two equations yields ( $y_A=0.44$, $x_A=0.26$).
  • This illustrates that small variations of the relative volatility are acceptable when assuming a constant relative volatility (and the system is ideal!).
