Multi-Stage Evaporators: Design Procedure

Step 0-a: Draw A Process Flowsheet

  • We are going to cover the example design in section 8.5D of “Transport Processes and Unit Operations,” by C. J. Geankoplis. As the book is not the easiest to get ahold of, it is repeated here.
  • There are another two examples of Multi-Stage Evaporator design available in C&R Volume 2, if you wish to study the topic further.
  • This design concerns a triple-effect evaporator in a white-sugar factory.
  • The design procedure, if followed correctly, will yield a valid process design in all cases and satisfy the implicit mass and energy equations.
  • The reason for following the procedure rigorously is to ensure that our calculations converge onto the solution. There are, in-fact, many ways of solving this system.

Step 1: Solve the Final Evaporator

  • As we know the final concentration, the boiling point rise in the third evaporator is \begin{align*} \textrm{BPR}&=1.78\times0.5+6.22\times0.5^2\approx2.45 \textrm{${}^\circ $C/K} \end{align*}
  • We then look up the boiling point of pure water at 0.134 bar (324.82 K) and calculate the operating temperature \begin{align*} T_3&=324.82+2.45=327.27\textrm{K} \end{align*}

Step 1: Solve the Final Evaporator

  • Using a mass balance in the solids, we can determine the outlet liquid flow-rate. \begin{align*} x_F F &= x_3 L_3\\ 0.1\times22680 &= 0.5\times L_3\\ L_3 &= 0.1\times22680 / 0.5 = 4536\textrm{ kg/hr} \end{align*}

Step 2: Predict the Vapour Flowrates

  • Now we can calculate the total vapour flow-rate using the overall mass balance \begin{align*} F &= L_3 + V_1 + V_2 + V_3\\ 22680&=4536 + V_1 + V_2 + V_3\\ V_1 + V_2 + V_3 &= 18144\textrm{ kg/hr} \end{align*}

Step 2: Predict the Vapour Flowrates

  • Here is the start of our iterations/shooting: we assume an equal amount of vapour is generated in each phase: \begin{align*} V_1\approx V_2\approx V_3\approx 18144 / 3 =6048\textrm{ kg/hr} \end{align*}
  • It is important to remember that this is an estimate, and it may need to be improved upon!

Step 3: Predict the Liquid Flowrates

  • Now, using the vapour flow-rate estimates, $V$, we can perform mass balances on each stage to calculate the liquid flowrates. \begin{align*} L_1 &= F - V_1 = 22680 - 6048 = 16632 \textrm{ kg/hr}\\ L_2 &= L_1 - V_2 = 16632 - 6048 = 10584 \textrm{ kg/hr} \end{align*}

Step 4: Predict the Concentrations

  • With the flowrates predicted, we can estimate the concentrations using solids mass balances around each stage, and validate our calculations by checking the last stage's solids balance. (Remember to check your calculations!) \begin{align*} x_1 &= x_F F/L_1 = 0.1\times22680/16632 &&\approx 0.136\\ x_2 &= x_1 L_1/L_2 = 0.136\times16632/10584 &&\approx 0.214\\ x_3 &= x_2 L_2/L_3 = 0.214\times10584/4536 &&\approx \color{green}{0.500 \qquad\checkmark} \end{align*}

Step 4: Calculate the Operating Temperatures

  • With all of the information we have so far, we are ready to predict the operating temperatures.
  • The concentrations allow us to determine the boiling point rises, and we can use the following equation to predict the temperature drops: \begin{align*} \Delta T_1 \approx \frac{1}{U_1}\frac{\sum_i\Delta T_i}{\sum_i1/U_i} \end{align*}

Step 4: Calculate the Operating Temperatures

  • There is a difficulty when using this equation when a Boiling Point Rise (BPR) occurs.
  • The vapour leaving a stage, $i$, is superheated. When it is used to heat the next stage, it will actually heat at $T_i-\textrm{BPR}_i$, as the small superheat is negligible.
  • Thus, when we have a BPR in a triple effect evaporator, we actually write \begin{align*} \sum_i\Delta T_i = T_{steam} - T_3 - \textrm{BPR}_1 - \textrm{BPR}_2 \end{align*}
  • This is the actual temperature difference available for heat transfer.

Step 4: Calculate the Operating Temperatures

  • We must also be careful to note that each temperature difference is defined as follows: \begin{align*} \Delta T_1 &= T_{steam} - T_1\\ \Delta T_2 &= T_{1} - \textrm{BPR}_1 - T_2\\ \Delta T_3 &= T_{2} - \textrm{BPR}_2 - T_3\\ … \end{align*}
  • We are ready to calculate the temperature differences…

Step 4: Calculate the Operating Temperatures

  • The saturation temperature of the 2.055 bar steam heating the first evaporator is 394.25 K (from steam tables).
  • The boiling point rises in stages 1 and 2 are \begin{align*} \textrm{BPR}_1&=1.78\times0.136+6.22\times0.136^2\approx0.36 \textrm{${}^\circ $C/K}\\ \textrm{BPR}_2&=1.78\times0.214+6.22\times0.214^2\approx0.65 \textrm{${}^\circ $C/K} \end{align*}
  • We can now calculate the overall available temperature difference…

Step 4: Calculate the Operating Temperatures

  • The overall temperature difference is \begin{align*} \sum_i\Delta T_i &= T_{steam} - T_3 - \textrm{BPR}_1 - \textrm{BPR}_2\\ &= 394.25-327.27-0.36-0.65 = 65.97 \textrm{K} \end{align*}
  • We can now calculate the temperature difference in each stage…

Step 4: Calculate the Operating Temperatures

For the first and second stages, the temperature difference is \begin{align*} \Delta T_1 &= T_{steam} - T_1 \approx \frac{1}{U_1}\frac{\sum_i\Delta T_i}{\sum_i1/U_i} \approx 12.40 K \\ \Delta T_2 &= T_1 - \textrm{BPR}_1 - T_2 \approx \frac{1}{U_2}\frac{\sum_i\Delta T_i}{\sum_i1/U_i}\approx 19.50 K \end{align*}

Step 4: Calculate the Operating Temperatures

  • We must note that this is an approximate expression \begin{align*} \Delta T_i &\approx \frac{1}{U_i}\frac{\sum_i\Delta T_i}{\sum_i1/U_i} \end{align*}
  • The actual temperature differences will also depend on the sensible heat changes in the feed and between stages.

Step 4: Calculate the Operating Temperatures

  • We note that the feed stream is quite cold and not likely to be at its boiling point.
  • Therefore, we make an educated guess (I admit it, I'm cheating) that the temperature difference needs to be higher in the first stage than predicted. \begin{align*} \Delta T_1&\approx 12.40 K\to 15.56 K & \Delta T_2&\approx 19.50 K\to18.34 K\\ \Delta T_3&\approx 34.07 K \to 32.07 K \end{align*}

Step 4: Calculate the Operating Temperatures

  • As a first estimate we've increased $\Delta T_1$ and lower $\Delta T_2$ and $\Delta T_3$ proportionately.
  • We must do this proportionately as the overall temperature difference is an exact definition. \begin{align*} \sum_i\Delta T_i = T_{steam} - T_3 - \textrm{BPR}_1 - \textrm{BPR}_2 \end{align*}

Step 4: Calculate the Operating Temperatures

  • Using these temperature differences, and the BPR for each stage, we can calculate the operating temperatures. \begin{align*} \Delta T_1 &= T_s - T_1 \approx 15.56 K & T_1 \approx 378.69 \textrm{K}\\ \Delta T_2 &= T_1 - \textrm{BPR}_1 - T_2 \approx 18.34 K & T_2 \approx 359.99 \textrm{K} \end{align*}

Step 5: Heat Balances on Each Effect

  • Now that we have (estimated) temperatures for the evaporators, its time to confirm our estimates for the vapour and liquid flowrates (and turn those orange values black).
  • We can calculate the enthalpies for each stream using the steam tables.
  • As the BPR is quite small, we can probably neglect the superheat of the vapour streams.

Step 5: Heat Balances on Each Effect

  • If we create our energy balances using these assumptions, they will look like this \begin{align*} h_{F} F + Q_{steam} &= h_{V,1} V_1 + h_{L,1} L_1\\ h_{L,1} L_1 + h_{fg,V_1} V_1 &= h_{V,2} V_2 + h_{L,2} L_2\\ h_{L,2} L_2 + h_{fg,V_2} V_2 &= h_{V,3} V_3 + h_{L,3} L_3 \end{align*}
  • We can substitute these general relationships for the vapour flow-rates to eliminate the vapour terms \begin{align*} V_1 &= F - L_1 & V_2 &= L_1 - L_2\\ V_3 &= L_2 - L_3 \end{align*}
  • And we end up with three unknowns ($Q_{steam}$, $L_1$, and $L_2$) with three equations: \begin{align*} h_{F} F + Q_{steam} &= h_{V,1} \left(F-L_1\right) + h_{L,1} L_1\\ h_{L,1} L_1 + h_{fg,V_1} V_1 &= h_{V,2} \left(L_1-L_2\right) + h_{L,2} L_2\\ h_{L,2} L_2 + h_{fg,V_2} V_2 &= h_{V,3} \left(L_2-L_3\right) + h_{L,3} L_3 \end{align*}

Step 5: Heat Balances on Each Effect

  • If we substitute in the known values, we can solve for the liquid and vapour flow rates (see the attached notes for all stages). \begin{align*} L_1 &= 17078\textrm{ kg/hr}& L_2 &= 11068\textrm{ kg/hr}& Q_{steam} &= 19.66\textrm{ MJ/hr}\\ V_1 &= 5602\textrm{ kg/hr}& V_2 &= 6010\textrm{ kg/hr}& V_3 &= 6532\textrm{ kg/hr} \end{align*}
  • Comparing this to the previous calculations, we can see we're within 10% of the estimated vapour flow rates.

Step 5: Heat Balances on Each Effect

  • If we were off with our initial estimates by more than 10%, then we would have to take these newly calculated values and recalculate the concentrations in each evaporator again.
  • However, as this is within 10% we can carry on with the design (using these new values for $V_1$, $V_2$, $V_3$).

Step 5: Heat Balances on Each Effect

  • With the mass balance “solved”, we can perform an enthalpy balance on each stage to calculate the heat transfer required. \begin{align*} Q_1 &= 5.46 \text{MW} & Q_2 &= 3.49 \text{MW} & Q_3 &= 3.83 \text{MW} \end{align*}

Step 6: Confirm evaporator area

  • Given the overall heat transfer coefficients from a manufacturer ($U_1=3123$ W m ${}^{-2}$ K ${}^{-1}$, $U_1=1987$ W m ${}^{-2}$ K ${}^{-1}$, $U_1=1136$ W m ${}^{-2}$ K ${}^{-1}$), we can calculate the areas as \begin{align*} A_1 &= 112.4 \text{m}^2 & A_2 &= 95.8 \text{m}^2 & A_3 &= 105.1 \text{m}^2 \end{align*}
  • The average area is $A_m=104.4\,\text{m}^2$ and, as all values are within 10% of this average, our design is complete.

Step 6: Confirm evaporator area

  • If the areas were significantly different, we would have to revise our estimated temperature drops, $\Delta T_1$, $\Delta T_2$, $\Delta T_3$.
  • We can now go and calculate the stage pressures and, using an enthalpy balance, the steam required.
  • We can also calculate the steam economy.
